Section 1 — Purpose and Scope

The purpose of this Snow and Ice Management Policy is to establish clear procedures and responsibilities for maintaining safe conditions on the premises of {{ business_name }} during winter weather events. Accumulation of snow and ice on walkways, parking areas, and building entrances poses a significant risk of slip-and-fall injuries to employees, customers, vendors, and visitors.

This policy is designed to minimize that risk through proactive monitoring, timely removal of snow and ice, proper documentation of all actions taken, and clear assignment of duties. Compliance with this policy demonstrates a commitment to safety and may serve as evidence of reasonable care in the event of a liability claim.

Scope. This policy applies to all exterior areas of the business premises, including but not limited to:

  • Primary and secondary entrances and exits
  • All exterior walkways and sidewalks
  • ADA-accessible routes and ramps
  • Customer and employee parking areas
  • Loading docks and delivery areas
  • Stairways, handrails, and elevated walkways

Section 2 — Responsibilities

2.1   Management

  • Ensure this policy is distributed to all employees and that each employee signs the acknowledgment page.
  • Maintain an adequate inventory of snow and ice removal supplies before and throughout the winter season.
  • Designate specific employees responsible for monitoring weather conditions and performing removal duties.
  • Arrange and oversee any third-party snow removal contracts, ensuring contractors carry adequate insurance.
  • Review incident reports promptly and take corrective action as warranted.

2.2   Designated Employees

  • Monitor local weather forecasts before and during business hours.
  • Perform snow and ice removal in accordance with the procedures outlined in Section 4.
  • Complete a Snow and Ice Activity Log entry for every action taken (see Section 7).
  • Inspect priority areas at least every two hours during active precipitation events.
  • Report any equipment shortages or maintenance needs to management immediately.

2.3   All Staff

  • Report hazardous conditions (ice, snow accumulation, wet floors near entrances) to a designated employee or manager as soon as observed.
  • Exercise caution when walking on exterior surfaces during winter weather.
  • Use designated entrances that have been cleared and treated whenever possible.
  • Do not attempt snow or ice removal unless trained and assigned to do so.

Section 3 — Weather Monitoring

Proactive weather monitoring is the first line of defense against snow and ice hazards. The designated employee(s) shall:

  1. Pre-shift check. Review the local forecast at least 30 minutes before the start of business hours each day from November through March (or as conditions warrant).
  2. Ongoing monitoring. Check conditions at a minimum of every two hours during business hours when temperatures are at or below 35°F (2°C), or when precipitation is occurring or forecast.
  3. Refreezing awareness. Be alert to temperature drops in the late afternoon and evening that may cause melted snow or standing water to refreeze, particularly on shaded surfaces and metal grates.
  4. Storm preparation. When significant winter weather is forecast (3+ inches of snow, ice storms, or freezing rain), ensure all equipment and supplies are staged and accessible before the event begins.

Recommended sources:

  • National Weather Service — weather.gov
  • Local television and radio weather reports
  • Commercial weather applications (e.g., Weather Channel, AccuWeather, Dark Sky)
  • State Department of Transportation winter weather advisories

Section 4 — Snow and Ice Removal Procedures

4.1   Trigger Conditions

Removal operations shall begin when any of the following conditions are observed:

  • Snow accumulation reaches 1 inch or more on any walkway, entrance, or pedestrian surface.
  • Any ice formation is present on walking surfaces, ramps, or stairs.
  • Freezing rain is actively falling or forecast within the next two hours.
  • A slip or near-miss incident is reported on the premises.

4.2   Priority Areas

Removal shall be performed in the following order of priority:

Priority Area Rationale
1 — Critical Main entrance and ADA-accessible routes Highest foot traffic; accessibility compliance
2 — High Secondary exits and emergency egress paths Fire code and life-safety requirements
3 — High Customer walkways and sidewalks Public-facing liability exposure
4 — Medium Loading docks and delivery areas Vendor and employee safety
5 — Medium Parking lot and driving lanes Vehicle and pedestrian safety

4.3   Removal Steps

  1. Begin with the main entrance. Clear snow from the door threshold outward to a width of at least 48 inches.
  2. Shovel all walkways to full width. Push snow to the side away from the walking path; do not allow snow piles to obstruct sight lines or drainage.
  3. Apply de-icing material evenly to all cleared surfaces per the guidelines in Section 5.
  4. Check for ice underneath snow cover. Scrape ice with an ice scraper and apply de-icer directly.
  5. Place "Caution — Wet/Slippery" signs at entrances and any areas where residual moisture is present.
  6. Re-inspect and re-treat all priority areas at least every 2 hours during active precipitation.
  7. After precipitation ends, perform a final clearing pass and document completion in the Activity Log.

4.4   Parking Lot

If a third-party contractor is responsible for parking lot plowing and treatment, refer to the current contractor agreement for service triggers, response times, and scope. Management shall verify contractor performance after each event.

If parking lot maintenance is handled by staff, follow the same removal steps above. Use a push-style snow shovel or mechanical snow blower for large areas. Apply de-icer to pedestrian crossings, handicapped spaces, and high-traffic lanes first.

Section 5 — De-Icing Materials and Application

5.1   Approved Materials

Material Effective Temperature Notes
Rock salt (sodium chloride) Down to 15°F (−9°C) Most common; cost-effective. Less effective in extreme cold.
Calcium chloride Down to −25°F (−32°C) Effective in very cold conditions. Generates heat on contact.
Magnesium chloride Down to 0°F (−18°C) Less corrosive than calcium chloride. Safer near vegetation.
Sand / grit All temperatures Provides traction only; does not melt ice. Use on slopes and ramps.

5.2   Application Guidelines

  • Spread de-icing material evenly across the surface. A thin, uniform layer is more effective than heavy clumps.
  • Focus application on slopes, ramps, transitions between surfaces, and areas that tend to accumulate ice (shaded spots, downspouts, roof drip lines).
  • Avoid excessive application near landscaping and vegetation, as chloride-based products can damage plants and soil.
  • Apply material after shoveling, not before, for maximum effectiveness. Pre-treatment (anti-icing) may be applied before a forecast event at management's discretion.
  • Wear gloves when handling all de-icing chemicals.

5.3   Storage

  • Store all de-icing materials in a dry, covered, and easily accessible location.
  • Keep containers sealed to prevent moisture absorption and clumping.
  • Verify inventory levels monthly from October through March; reorder when supply falls below 50 lbs.

Section 7 — Documentation Requirements

Thorough documentation is essential for demonstrating due diligence and may be critical in defending against liability claims. A Snow and Ice Activity Log entry must be completed for every removal action, without exception.

7.1   Required Log Information

Each log entry shall include all of the following:

  • Date of action
  • Time of action (start and end)
  • Weather conditions at the time (temperature, precipitation type and intensity, wind)
  • Actions taken (shoveling, scraping, de-icer application, sign placement, etc.)
  • Areas addressed (list specific locations from the priority table)
  • Materials used and approximate quantity
  • Employee name of the person performing the work

7.2   Retention

  • All Snow and Ice Activity Logs shall be retained for a minimum of 3 years from the date of entry.
  • Logs should be stored in a secure, organized manner (physical binder or digital system).
  • Management shall review log completeness on a monthly basis during the winter season.

7.3   Photographic Documentation

  • When practical, take date- and time-stamped photographs of cleared areas after each removal action.
  • Photograph any conditions that could not be fully remediated (e.g., persistent ice on an uneven surface).
  • Store photographs with the corresponding log entry.
Note: SafeWalk provides a digital logging system that automatically records date, time, weather conditions, and photographs. Using the SafeWalk platform satisfies all documentation requirements outlined in this section.

Section 8 — Emergency Procedures

8.1   If Someone Falls or Is Injured

  1. Do not move the person unless they are in immediate danger (e.g., in a traffic lane).
  2. Call 911 immediately if the person is seriously injured, unconscious, or unable to stand.
  3. Secure the area around the injured person. Place caution signs or cones to redirect foot traffic.
  4. Document conditions at the scene: take photographs of the exact fall location, note weather conditions, and identify any ice or snow accumulation present.
  5. Notify management immediately, regardless of the severity of the incident.
  6. Provide first aid only if trained and certified to do so.

8.2   Incident Reporting

  • Complete a written Incident Report within 24 hours of any slip, fall, or injury on the premises related to snow or ice.
  • Include: date, time, location, conditions, witness information, actions taken, and injury description.
  • Preserve all Snow and Ice Activity Log entries for the 48 hours preceding the incident.
  • Preserve all photographic documentation from the day of the incident.
  • Do not discard, alter, or overwrite any records related to the incident.
  • Notify your insurance carrier as required by your policy terms.

Section 9 — Annual Review

This policy shall be reviewed and updated before each winter season. The annual review should be completed no later than October 1 of each year and shall include:

  1. Policy review. Read through the entire policy and update any procedures that have changed or proved inadequate during the prior season.
  2. Contact information. Verify and update all emergency contacts, designated employee assignments, and contractor details.
  3. Equipment inventory. Conduct the equipment and supplies checklist (Section 6) and order any items that are missing, damaged, or below minimum quantities.
  4. Contractor agreements. Review and renew any third-party snow removal contracts. Confirm insurance certificates are current.
  5. Employee training. Conduct a brief training session or meeting to review procedures with all employees. Distribute updated copies of this policy.
  6. Lessons learned. Review any incidents from the prior season and incorporate corrective actions into updated procedures.

Retain prior versions of this policy for reference. Document the date of each review and the name of the person who conducted it.
